Overview of active and planned rockets

1.1 Variance in payload fairing configurations

Fig1.

Fig2.

Insights:

  • Different configurations of rockets serve different purposes for different entities.
  • SpaceX owns the rockets of the largest average payloads likely in order to make full use of each launch and improve the cost efficiency from a commercialization point of view. Similarly, Blue Origin, the other private company, also owns rockets with relatively large fairing size, although its average payload capacity isn’t as large as SpaceX.
  • In contrast, entities that do not operate for profits own rockets with smaller fairing size, likely because some rockets are designed for specialized purposes. For example, CASC (China Aerospace Science and Technology Corporation) owns the most number of rockets. CASC is a Chinese state-owned company responsible for all Chinese space programs, which serves a similar purpose as NASA. The average fairing size and average payloads of all its rockets are in the middle among all entities. NASA owns fewer rockets than CASC but its average fairing size is slightly larger.

1.2: Efficiency and Success Rate

Fig3.

Insights:

  • SpaceX is among the cost effective entity if we compare the average dollar cost to send 1 kilogram of payloads into either Low earth orbit (LEO) or Geostationary transfer orbit (GTO). Its cost effectiveness stands out most when we compare its ability to GTO. ROSCOSMOS (Russian government agency) is also competitive in terms of cost effectiveness. Boeing isn’t the most cost effective surprisingly, despite its leading position in aerospace.
  • NASA and CASC are among the least cost efficient in comparison since they are not for profits.

Fig4.

Insights:

  • No clear correlation of rocket prices and success rate.
  • Rockets and entities with more launches (larger shape size) generally have very high success rates. Rocket and space missions are highly costly and competitive, so it’s understandable that entities will go into outright failure if they cannot succeed at the first few launches or the rockets will be given up or rebuilt if they failed to complete missions.
  • One rocket of SpaceX shows impressive success rates of 99% with over 300 missions. All CASC rockets in our database show 96%-100% success rates, most of which have succeeded in dozens of missions. ULA, the US launch service provider, also shows over 99% success rate in 100+ missions.
